# Legal Research: Section 162 - Delinquency and Probation Orders

**Source:** Companies Act No. 71 of 2008 (South Africa)  
**Date Compiled:** November 4, 2025

---

## Section 162: Delinquency and Probation Orders

### Overview

Section 162 of the Companies Act No. 71 of 2008 provides for the declaration of directors as delinquent or the placing of directors under probation. These provisions are designed to protect companies, the public, and corporate stakeholders from directors who are unable or have proven unable to effectively manage the company.

---

## Grounds for Delinquency Declaration - Section 162(5)

### Mandatory Delinquency ("Court Must Declare")

A court **must** declare a director delinquent if the director:

#### 1. Ineligibility or Disqualification (Section 162(5)(a))
- Consents to serve as a director while **ineligible or disqualified**
- Fails to meet minimum qualification criteria to serve as a director

#### 2. Violation of Probation Order (Section 162(5)(b))
- Acts in violation of an existing probation order under the Companies Act
- Acts in violation of an existing probation order under the Close Corporations Act
- Demonstrates disregard for legal constraints

#### 3. Gross Abuse of Position (Section 162(5)(c))

**Section 162(5)(c)(i) - Gross Abuse:**
- Grossly abused the position of director
- **"Gross abuse"** denotes egregious conduct
- Must relate to the **use of the position** as director, not merely performance of duties
- Conduct must be of a very serious nature
- Goes beyond ordinary negligence

**Section 162(5)(c)(ii) - Personal Advantage:**
- Took personal advantage of information or opportunities
- Information or opportunities obtained in the capacity as director
- For personal gain or benefit

**Section 162(5)(c)(iii) - Gross Negligence, Wilful Misconduct, Breach of Trust:**
- Intentionally or by gross negligence inflicted harm upon the company
- Engaged in conduct amounting to:
  - **Gross negligence** in relation to duties to the company
  - **Wilful misconduct** in relation to duties to the company
  - **Breach of trust** in relation to duties to the company

**Section 162(5)(c)(iv) - Fraudulent Conduct:**

**(aa) Acting Without Authority:**
- Acted in the name of the company without authority

**(bb) Reckless Trading and Fraud:**
- Acquiesced in the carrying on of the company's business recklessly
- Was a party to an act or omission by the company despite knowing it was:
  - Calculated to defraud creditors, employees, or shareholders of the company
  - Calculated to defraud creditors, employees, or shareholders of another person
  - For any fraudulent purpose

#### 4. Repeated Non-Compliance (Section 162(5)(d))
- Repeatedly subject to compliance notices or similar enforcement mechanisms
- For similar conduct
- Has been at least twice:
  - Convicted of an offence
  - Subject to administrative fines
  - Subject to similar penalties

#### 5. Multiple Company Failures (Section 162(5)(e))
- Within the preceding five years:
  - Was a director of one or more companies
  - Was a managing member of one or more close corporations
  - Controlled or participated in control of a juristic person
- That juristic person was:
  - Convicted of an offence, or
  - Subjected to an administrative fine or other penalty

## Grounds for Probation Order - Section 162(7)

### Discretionary Probation ("Court May Order")

A court **may** place a director under probation if the director:

#### 1. Failure to Vote Against Resolution (Section 162(7)(a))
- Present at a meeting where resolution was considered
- Failed to vote against the resolution despite knowing:
  - The company did not satisfy the solvency and liquidity test
  - The resolution would likely cause the company to fail the test
- Constitutes failure to act in best interests of the company
- Exposes company to unnecessary financial risk

#### 2. Material Inconsistency with Duties (Section 162(7)(b))
- Acted in a manner **materially inconsistent** with duties as director
- Breach of fiduciary duties
- Failure to exercise care, skill, and diligence

#### 3. Oppressive or Unfairly Prejudicial Conduct (Section 162(7)(c))
- Acted in a manner determined to be:
  - **Oppressive**, or
  - **Unfairly prejudicial** to the company or any shareholder
- As per Section 163(1) of the Companies Act
- Supported a decision of the company to act in such manner

#### 4. Multiple Company Failures - Creditor Non-Payment (Section 162(7)(d))
- Within 10 years from 1 May 2011:
  - Was a director of more than one company, or
  - Was a managing member of more than one close corporation
- During that time, two or more of those entities:
  - Failed to fully pay all creditors, or
  - Failed to meet all obligations
- **Exception:** Does not apply if failure occurred:
  - In terms of a business rescue plan resulting from board resolution
  - In terms of a compromise with creditors under Section 155

## Procedural Requirements

### Who Can Lodge Application (Section 162(2))

An application for delinquency or probation may be brought by:
- The Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC)
- A company
- A shareholder or director of a company
- A member of a close corporation
- A trade union representing employees of the company
- Any other person specified in the Act

### Standard of Proof

- Delinquency declarations require proof of serious misconduct
- "Gross abuse" requires egregious conduct beyond ordinary negligence
- Conduct must be of a very serious nature
- Court exercises discretion in determining whether conduct meets threshold

---

## Consequences of Delinquency Order

### Minimum Ban Period
- **Seven years** from date of order (minimum)
- **Lifetime ban** in serious cases
- Director is prohibited from:
  - Serving as a director of any company
  - Being involved in management of any company
  - Acting as a trustee of a trust carrying on business

### Suspension and Setting Aside (Section 162(11))
- After **three years**, delinquent director may apply to court to:
  - Suspend the delinquency order
  - Substitute it with a probation order
- Court considers:
  - Conduct since delinquency order
  - Rehabilitation efforts
  - Public interest

---

## Application to Close Corporations

### Close Corporations Act No. 69 of 1984

The delinquency provisions apply **mutatis mutandis** (with necessary changes) to:
- Managing members of close corporations
- Members exercising management functions
- Similar grounds and procedures apply

### Allegations in Founding Affidavit

Peter alleges grounds under Section 162(5)(c):

1. **Gross Abuse of Position:**
   - Unauthorized IT expenses
   - Alleged misappropriation of funds
   - Interference with corporate operations

2. **Personal Advantage:**
   - Alleged R500,000 "birthday gift"
   - Unexplained IT expenses for personal benefit

   - Failure to provide invoices
   - Lack of documentation for expenses
   - Tax compliance issues

4. **Breach of Trust:**
   - Acting against interests of corporations
   - Enabling unauthorized transactions

### Defense Considerations

**Burden of Proof:**
- Peter must prove conduct meets "gross abuse" threshold
- Ordinary negligence or business disagreements insufficient
- Requires egregious conduct of very serious nature

**Material Non-Disclosures:**
- Failure to disclose Bantjies as third trustee
- Failure to disclose Bantjies' R18.685M debt to trust
- Failure to disclose conflict of interest
- May undermine entire application

**Alternative Explanations:**
- IT expenses necessary for R34.9M e-commerce operation
- Documentation provided on 6 June 2025
- Revenue diversion by Peter's agents (Rynette, Bantjies)
- Manufactured crisis through card cancellations

**Oppressive Conduct by Applicant:**
- Section 162(7)(c) may apply to Peter's own conduct
- Systematic sabotage of business operations
- Retaliatory actions against whistleblower (Daniel)
- Use of trust powers to attack beneficiary

## Key Legal Principles

1. **High Threshold:** Delinquency requires proof of serious, egregious conduct
2. **Context Matters:** Court considers full factual matrix and business context
3. **Material Non-Disclosure:** Can invalidate ex parte orders
4. **Oppression Remedies:** Available to directors facing retaliatory applications
5. **Fiduciary Duties:** Apply to all directors and trustees
6. **Good Faith:** Required in all corporate decision-making
